Heads up – that Microsoft sign-in alert was a phishing simulation.

We logged this event to help improve your awareness. Take a minute to review how this email tried to trick you and what to look for next time.

Red flags in this email

  • Suspicious sender address – it did not come from a real @outlook.com or @microsoft.com domain.
  • Urgent security warning – “unusual sign-in activity” with pressure to act immediately.
  • Generic greeting – “Dear user” instead of your real name.
  • Fake sign-in button – the button claimed to take you to Microsoft, but the URL pointed to a different website.
  • Formatting / layout issues – spacing, fonts and wording were slightly off compared with genuine Microsoft notices.

How to verify alerts like this safely

Open Microsoft account from your browser bookmarks – not the email
Check the sender domain carefully (full address, not just display name)
Hover over buttons/links to see where they really go
If unsure, contact IT or the Help Desk

Next time you see a sign-in alert…

  • Ask yourself: Was I expecting this? – real alerts usually follow activity you recognize.
  • Check for sign-ins directly from your Microsoft account security page instead of trusting the email.
  • Never enter your password or MFA code after clicking an unexpected link.

Do’s and Don’ts

Do:

  • Use multi-factor authentication (MFA) on your accounts.
  • Report suspicious messages using the “Report” button in the email.
  • Double-check account activity directly in the Microsoft portal.

Don’t:

  • Approve sign-in requests you did not start yourself.
  • Enter passwords on pages opened from unexpected emails.
  • Ignore unusual login alerts without verifying them through a trusted path.

Policy reminder

If you ever receive a real security alert:
  • Do not click links inside the email.
  • Go directly to the application (e.g., Microsoft 365) via your browser.
  • Report anything suspicious to IT Security immediately.
