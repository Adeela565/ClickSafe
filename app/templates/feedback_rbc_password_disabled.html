That RBC “password disabled” message was a phishing simulation.

Banking-themed phishing attacks are common. Let’s look at how this fake RBC message tried to get your credentials and what to do when you see similar emails.

Red flags in this email

  • Sender domain wasn’t an official RBC domain (for example, not @rbc.com or @rbcroyalbank.com).
  • Alarmist language – “your password has been disabled” with pressure to “sign in immediately.”
  • Embedded sign-in link – the button did not lead to the RBC website when you hover over it.
  • Missing personal details – no partial account number or other identifiers RBC usually includes.
  • Generic footer & legal text – formatting and wording didn’t match a real bank notice.

How to verify banking emails safely

Never click “sign in” from a banking email
Type the bank’s web address yourself or use a saved bookmark
Check for HTTPS and the correct domain before logging in
Call the number on the back of your card if you’re unsure

When you receive a real banking alert…

  • Use the official mobile app or website to review messages.
  • Enable account alerts (text/app) so you can cross-check suspicious emails.
  • Report unexpected password or account-lock emails to your bank’s fraud team.

Do’s and Don’ts

Do:

  • Store bank logins in a password manager, not your browser.
  • Use MFA for your online banking accounts.
  • Immediately report suspected fraud to your bank and IT Security.

Don’t:

  • Enter banking credentials after clicking an email link.
  • Trust emails that threaten account closure or legal action if you don’t act “now.”
  • Forward suspected phishing to colleagues; report it instead.

Policy reminder

For any suspected compromise:
  • Contact your bank using a trusted phone number or app.
  • Notify your organization’s IT Security team immediately.
  • Change passwords from a clean, trusted device.
