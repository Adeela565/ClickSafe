That “delivery notice” email was a phishing simulation.

Package-delivery scams are popular because we order so many things online. This simulation showed how attackers can use fake courier messages to get you to click.

Red flags in this email

  • Unexpected package – you weren’t aware of a delivery for your name or office.
  • Vague tracking information – no real order number, vendor, or shipment details.
  • URL didn’t match the real courier site when hovering over “Track package” or “Pay duty.”
  • Sender domain looked similar but not exact (for example @ups-support.com instead of @ups.com).
  • Request for payment or personal details to release the package.

How to verify delivery emails

Check your real order history (e.g., Amazon, vendor portal)
Use official courier apps or websites to track packages
Do not pay fees from email links
Ask the sender/vendor to confirm tracking information

When in doubt about a package…

  • Search for the tracking number on the courier’s official website, not via the email link.
  • Be suspicious of emails asking you to install apps or browser extensions to track shipments.
  • Never provide credit card details or SIN/SSN to “release” a delivery.

Do’s and Don’ts

Do:

  • Use multi-factor authentication on shopping accounts.
  • Keep a record of your recent orders to cross-check emails.
  • Report suspicious courier emails right away.

Don’t:

  • Click “reschedule delivery” links if you weren’t expecting a package.
  • Scan QR codes or open attachments from unknown delivery notices.
  • Share personal information just to “confirm” a shipment.

Policy reminder

For work-related deliveries:
  • Follow your organization’s standard shipping and receiving process.
  • Do not reroute packages or pay “fees” from personal accounts.
  • Report suspicious delivery emails to IT Security and Facilities if relevant.
