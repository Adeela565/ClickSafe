That “payroll update” message was a phishing simulation.

Attackers know people pay attention to salary and tax messages. This simulation showed how an attacker could try to steal your login using a fake HR or payroll email.

Red flags in this email

  • Unexpected pay change – mention of a “salary adjustment” or “bonus confirmation” you weren’t expecting.
  • Request to log in urgently to confirm details before payroll is processed.
  • Link did not point to the real HR/Payroll system when you hover over it.
  • Sender address not matching HR/payroll domains your organization actually uses.
  • Unusual tone or grammar for a formal HR communication.

How to verify HR or payroll emails

Open the HR/Payroll portal from your bookmarks or intranet
Check internal announcements or Teams/HR channels
Contact HR directly if something looks wrong
Do not approve changes you didn’t request

Whenever money or tax is involved…

  • Be extra cautious of links and attachments related to pay, bonuses, T4s, or tax refunds.
  • Expect HR to use consistent templates and wording; odd formatting can be a red flag.
  • Never send SIN/SSN or banking details by email in response to a message.

Do’s and Don’ts

Do:

  • Enable MFA on any HR/payroll system you access.
  • Use strong, unique passwords for your work accounts.
  • Report suspicious payroll emails immediately.

Don’t:

  • Click “view new paystub” links from unexpected emails.
  • Share pay or banking details with senders you can’t verify.
  • Ignore small signs like misspelled company names or wrong logos.

Policy reminder

Real HR changes should be:
  • Visible in your official HR/Payroll portal.
  • Communicated through approved channels (HR emails, intranet, meetings).
  • Confirmed if anything seems unexpected or suspicious.
